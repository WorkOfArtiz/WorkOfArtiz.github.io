Domain I: Touchscreen (Mobile or Tablet) as Second Screen for watching TV

There is current trend for people to use the TV and the Web simultaneously using various devices, such as phones, laptops and tablets. These âsecond screenâ user experiences have some drawbacks, for example, people are having to focus on more than one screen at a time, their attention divided between the two. Also the ergonomics can be awkward; with laptops balanced on knees, looking down on tablets or smart phones, no haptic feedback from touch devices and thumbs typing on tiny keyboards. Moreover, when youâre following an online conversation about a programme you want to be able to comment quickly, so the act of typing canât be too difficult or distracting. In addition to making it easier to read and input text, second screens have several other user experience advantages over a single integrated Web/TV screen. For example, they:

  • Donât take the user away from main viewing experience
  • Donât clutter the TV screen with graphics/widgets
  • Allow for the potential for personalised interfaces: in most cases, itâs my social network but our TV, so displaying personal social network comments on the TV screen, as well as being a usability nightmare, is also a privacy problem.

Recently broadcasters and Internet start-ups have started to design Social TV apps specifically for second screen and remote controls TV experiences. Several apps and sites (such as Netflix, Hulu, Philo, Miso, and GetGlue) have repurposed the check-in concept popularised by Foursquare, Facebook and many other social media aps and adapted it for TV for a shared television watching experience. Other services are concentrating on integration with Twitter and Facebook. However, it is not clear at the moment what these second screen applications give the user that existing tools (such as using Twitter or Facebook on a laptop or phone) do not.

You are a designer team and you have to design an interface and interaction on a touchscreen used as a second screen before, after or while watching TV (for examples see below). The goal of the second screen is to provide a personal space for the TV viewers to interact with the TV while watching, e.g. (1) participate in online conversations about a TV programme, so that viewers in different places can share and discuss their thoughts, (2) play along: e.g.Â by real-time voting and predictions (âWhich contestant will win?â), answering trivia questions etc. (3) navigate to content: e.g.Â browsing through an archive collection and selecting a programme to watch, perhaps based on social recommendations, (4) access supplementary information about a programme (synchronised to the broadcast). Additionally, the second screen tablet could be used to also control the TV: i.e.Â using the second screen as a remote control to actually change whatâs happening on the TV screen.

In order to inspire yourself and gather background information about what features and functionality such a second screen table should have, you can explore features in existing applications as second screen (see examples below).

Domain II: Smartwatches as an Add on Screen to Listen Music

Wearable computers (i.e.Â wearables) are typically developed to support users in physical environment. They can provide a constant interaction between the computer and user by sensing the environment (i.e.Â user context), thus often there is no need to turn the device on or off or to start a particular application. They also support multitasking, by allowing you to continue what you were doing while using the device (which is seamlessly augmented into the usersâ context). Until recently, most of those devices were primarily developed and used either in research settings, or in sci-fi movies (e.g.Â prosthetics as an extension of the userâs hands, arms, head or event mind). Their intrusiveness in terms of size, shape and need for continuous power and connectivity, typically made them unusable in general context. Recently, with the introduction of Google Glass and Samsung Galaxy Gear, Apple Watch and the whole range of smart watches available on the market, such devices become more popular in common use. However, with the increase in their popularity, more and more issues arise in the context of their use. For example, they could:

  • Distract the user from the current activity
  • Obstruct the view of the user
  • Be too slow to react to the usersâ request
  • Consume too much power - exhausting batteries fast
  • Be too big and too small at the same time
  • Be of a security risk

Recently different companies started developing mainstream wearables, e.g.Â activity trackers wristbands Fibit, Nike+ Fuelband and Jawbone UP. Smartwatches, extending the interaction with smart phones seem to get more and more popular, e.g.Â Samsun Gear, Moto 360, Apple Watch, and many more still in design stages. However, it is not clear what the best mode of interaction is with such devices, and how to measure usability.

The techniques you need for the assignment are covered in detail in the the slides of the lecture on Prototyping; study these slides carefully before starting the assignment.

Target results of the assignment

The result of the assignment should be an extension of the report produced during Assignment 1, It should contain the following new elements:

Paper-based prototype:

Construct a low-fidelity prototype of your UI design developed in Assignment 1. The prototype should be paper-based and use sketches and/or storyboards as main technique(s). The techniques you need are discussed in the lecture on prototyping (consult the slides). The results should be a number of photos or scans of the prototype, together with explanation of all the design decisions. You can also use a tool like Balsamiq or any of the other tools for low-fidelity prototyping in the Appendix list at the end of this assignment. The content diagrams developed in Assignment 1 are the main starting point for the prototype. .

Cognitive walkthrough:

Perform a cognitive walkthrough of the prototype for each of the tasks you identified. For each task ask one group member should act as evaluator. Ask the evaluator to âthink aloudâ; the other group members should record the remarks made. Analyse the results and summarize the positive and negative feedback you have collected during those sessions. Indicate what changes/updates you need to do as a result of the cognitive walkthrough. Add the data collected (e.g.Â notes made during think-aloud) as an appendix to the report.

Software-based mock-up prototype:

Construct now a revised version of your mock-up, taking the results of the cognitive walkthrough into account. Use for this mock-up a tool such as Powerpoint, HTML or some other visual editing tool you are familiar with. Check out the list of tools included in the Appendix at the end of this assignment. Include screenshots and URLs in the report. Give explanations of the prototype parts and the design choices/rational you followed in order to make them.

Heuristic evaluation:

Conduct a heuristic evaluation of the second prototype. Use the Nielsen heuristics of Table 26.1 as the baseline for the evaluation (see also the example evaluation in Box 26.1). Ask one person from one of the other teams to act as evaluator/inspector. Include a summary of the results in table-form (see Table 26.3) in the report. Discuss implications for the UI design. Include the data collection form (Table 26.2) as an appendix to the report.

Change history:

During prototyping you are likely find out that parts of your requirements description and/or conceptual design were missing/insufficient/wrong. This is not a bug but a feature! Adjust where needed the requirements and design (as documented in Assignment 1) and add a change history to the report. In this change history you keep track of updates in a tabular form: date, person, change made and reason for the change.

Assignment 3: HCI Evaluation

Purpose

The goal of this assignment is to prepare the final evaluation of your final prototype (you will not execute this evaluation). You will define an experimental design (including evaluation goals, metrics related to your requirements and use context from assignment 1) to assess the usability of your prototype (resulted from assignment 2).

The result of this assignment constitutes an extension and update of your assignment 1-2 report. In addition, you will prepare as part of this assignment a presentation of the end result of the assignments.

Reading material

Chapters 20-25 & 27 of the coursebook, plus additional material from the lectures 5 and 6.

Target results of the assignment

You are asked to prepare an experimental design for usability evaluation of your prototype. You should focus the evaluation on two tasks (the ones that you identified yourself in Assignment 1 and realized in the prototypes of Assignment 2).

For each task you should define the following:

  • Hypothesis: what do you want to test?
  • Variables involved: dependent, independent, controlled.
  • Constraints that need to be considered.
  • Type of data you want to collect to confirm/reject your hypotheses

Based on these hypotheses, make an overall experimental plan to test both hypotheses:

  • Selection of methods for data collection
  • Planning of data collection sessions, including tools that need to be used, number of users, and physical setting.
  • Select summarization techniques of results (e.g.Â mean for time spent, mean for number of steps).
  • Qualitative conclusions are you interested in, e.g. âWe want to see how satisfied the users are overall with the new interface.â
  • Quantitative conclusions are you interested in, e.g. âWe will check how much the users deviated from the desired (mean) for number of steps in using our system.â
  • Strategy for confirming/rejecting your hypotheses based on this summarized data, e.g.Â if the users make on the average 2 steps to fulfil the task, than our interface is â¦.â.
  • Make sure you include a rational for each of those steps

The final result should be an extension of the report of Assignment 2. If your analysis gives rise to changes in earlier parts of the report, then make a note of this in the Change history.

Tips

  • The list of initial requirements in assignment 1 (including the non-functional HCI requirements) and the additions from assignment 2 will have to be extended/adapted in this assignment as well
  • In principle, you could have a separate data-collection plan for all hypotheses. In practice, it is more effective to combine data collection, e.g.Â to use a survey to ask questions concerning multiple hypotheses. For this reason, the overall experimental plan is for all hypotheses. You should indicate which parts of the plan are targeted at which hypothesis, where relevant.

Presentation of the assignment results

In addition to the experimental design you should also prepare a presentation about the combined result of your assignments. The rooms for the presentations can be found in the schedule (at the time of the last lab session).

You are asked to prepare a presentation of max 7 slides, according to the following structure:
